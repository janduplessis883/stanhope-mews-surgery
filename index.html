Childhood Vaccination FAQ [cite: 1]

It is entirely normal to have questions regarding medication for your child, and vaccines are no exception[cite: 2]. Below are answers to the most common questions parents ask[cite: 3].

Why should I have my child vaccinated? [cite: 4]

A common question is whether "herd immunity" is enough to protect a child. However, herd immunity does not protect against all diseases[cite: 5]. A primary example is tetanus, which is contracted from bacteria in the environment rather than from other infected individuals[cite: 6].

Furthermore, for herd immunity to be effective, a high percentage of the population must be vaccinated[cite: 7]. Because vaccination rates vary across different UK communities and overseas, an unvaccinated child is likely to encounter others who are also unvaccinated[cite: 8, 9]. This allows infectious diseases, such as measles, to spread rapidly through unvaccinated groups[cite: 10].

Will multiple vaccines overload my baby's immune system? [cite: 11]

Many parents worry about their child's ability to cope with several vaccines at once[cite: 12]. In reality, even a tiny baby's immune system handles this easily[cite: 13]. From birth, infants interact with millions of germs daily[cite: 14].

It is estimated that the human body contains enough white blood cells to handle thousands of vaccines simultaneously[cite: 15]. Giving 11 vaccines at once would only utilize about a thousandth of the immune system[cite: 16].

We do not recommend delaying vaccinations to "spread the load," as this leaves your child unprotected against serious diseases for a longer period[cite: 17].

How do I know that vaccines are safe? [cite: 18]

All vaccines undergo a rigorous development and testing process before being licensed[cite: 19]. They are tested on adults and children separately because vaccines effective in adults may perform differently in children[cite: 20, 21]. Crucially, no vaccine is tested on children until it has been fully tested on adults[cite: 22].
