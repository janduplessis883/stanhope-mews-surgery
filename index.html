Childhood Vaccination FAQ

It is normal to have questions about medication given to your child, and vaccines are no exception[cite: 2]. Here are answers to the most common questions[cite: 3].

Why should I have my child vaccinated?

Herd immunity does not protect against all diseases[cite: 5]. For example, tetanus is caught from bacteria in the environment rather than from other people[cite: 6].

For herd immunity to work, most people need to be vaccinated[cite: 7]. Low vaccination rates in some UK communities and overseas mean unvaccinated children are likely to encounter others without protection[cite: 8, 9]. This allows infectious diseases to spread quickly, as seen in the 2013 measles outbreak in Wales[cite: 10].

Immune System Capacity

Even a tiny baby's immune system can easily cope with several vaccines at once[cite: 13]. From birth, babies come into contact with millions of germs every day[cite: 14].

1/1000th Effort

It is estimated that giving 11 vaccines at once would only use about a thousandth of a child's immune system capacity[cite: 16]. Spreading out the load is not recommended as it leaves children unprotected for longer[cite: 17].

Safety & Testing Standards

All vaccines undergo long and thorough development and testing before being licensed[cite: 19]. They are tested on adults and children separately because vaccines can work differently in different age groups[cite: 20, 21]. Crucially, no vaccines are tested on children before being fully tested on adults[cite: 22].
